Tax guide for volunteers

Types of income, deductions, credits and expenses

  • Line 10100 Employment income (T4)

    • Individuals with employment income will receive an annual T4 slip, Statement of Remuneration Paid, from their employer(s).
    • The employment income is shown in box 14.
    • Other amounts shown on a T4 slip may entitle an individual to claim certain credits and deductions.

    Ufile tip

    The software will automatically claim all credits and deductions the individual is entitled to.

    Screen by screen instructions: Employment income (T4)

  • Line 10400 Other employment income (Tips) (Gratuities)

    • Employment income, such as tips, gratuities, or occasional earnings, may not be shown on a T4 slip. If they are not included on the T4 slip, it is the individual’s responsibility to provide you with the amounts.
    • All tips, gratuities and occasional earnings are taxable. The amount earned from January 1 through December 31 must be reported in the tax return.
    • Tips, gratuities and occasional earnings that are not included on a T4 slip are not subject to CPP contributions or EI premiums. However, an individual can choose to make CPP contributions on tips and gratuities.

    Screen by screen instructions: Other employment income (Tips) (Gratuities)

    • The amount of Old age Security received is shown in box 18.
    • This amount is not eligible for the Pension income amount on line 31400 nor the Elected split-pension amount on lines 11600 and 21000.

  • Line 11400 CPP or QPP benefits (T4A(P))

    • Canada Pension Plan (CPP) or Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) payments are taxable.
    • They are shown in box 20 of the T4A(P) slip and include the following amounts:
      • CPP or QPP retirement benefit (box 14)
      • CPP or QPP survivor benefit (box 15)
      • CPP or QPP disability benefit (box 16)
      • CPP or QPP post-retirement benefit (box 19)
    • These amounts are not eligible for the Pension income amount on line 31400 nor the Elected split-pension amount on lines 11600 and 21000.

  • Line 11500 Other pensions and superannuation (T4A)

    • Pensions, superannuation and annuity payments are taxable.
    • They are shown in boxes 016, 024, 133 and 194 of a T4A slip.
    • The amount from boxes 024, 133 and 194 are reported on line 11500 if one of the following applies:
      • the individual is 65 years of age or older on December 31
      • regardless of their age, received the amount upon the death of their spouse or common-law partner (the individual must provide the volunteer this information)
    • If the previous two situations do not apply, the amount is reported on line 13000.
    • The amounts on line 11500 qualify for the Elected split-pension income with a spouse on lines 11600 and 21000.
    • The software will automatically report the amounts on the appropriate line with the information provided.
    • The software will automatically claim up to $2,000 on line 31400 Pension income amount, if the individual qualifies for this amount.

    Screen by screen instructions: Other pensions and superannuation (T4A)

  • Line 11500 Other pensions and superannuation (T4RIF)

    • Payments from a registered retirement income fund (RRIF) are taxable.
    • They are shown in boxes 016 and 024 of a T4RIF slip.
    • These amounts are reported on line 11500 if:
      • the individual is 65 years of age or older on December 31, or
      • regardless of their age, received the amount upon the death of their spouse or common-law partner (the individual must provide the volunteer this information)
    • If the previous two situations do not apply, the amount is reported on line 13000.
    • The amounts on line 11500 qualify for the Elected split-pension income with a spouse on lines 11600 and 21000.
    • The software will automatically report the amounts on the appropriate line with the information provided.
    • The software will automatically claim up to $2,000 on line 31400 Pension income amount, if the individual qualifies for this amount.

    Complex situation

    • Volunteers should not complete tax returns for the following complex tax situations:
      • amounts appear in boxes other than 16, 20, 24, 28, 35 or 37,
      • box 26 is ticked or if box 32 has a SIN, it is too complex for the CVITP

  • Line 11500 Other pensions and superannuation (US Social security)

    • This income is taxable and must be reported on the tax return in Canadian dollars.
    • Individuals who receive United States social security benefits usually receive an SSA-1042-S-Social Security Benefit Statement.
      • The individual may be eligible for a 50% deduction on line 25600, if they are a resident of Canada and received the benefits continuously during the period starting before January 1, 1996 and ending in 2021.
      • 15% deduction otherwise
      • the individual will need to confirm the date they began to receive these benefits.
    • Eligible amounts may qualify for the Elected split-pension income with a spouse on lines 11600 and 21000.
    • The amount may be eligible for the Pension income amount on line 31400.
    • A tax return involving any other type of foreign pension income is not considered a simple tax situation.
    • The software will automatically claim the Pension income amount if eligible.

  • Line 11600 Elected split-pension amount

    • Pension income splitting is a joint election that allows an individual to transfer up to 50% of their eligible pension income to their spouse or common-law partner. This may allow the individual and their spouse or common-law partner to reduce their total amount of tax owed.
    • The spouse receiving the transferred amount will have the income reported on line 11600.
    • The spouse making the transfer will get a deduction for the transferred amount, on line 21000.
    • Form T1032, Joint Election to Split Pension Income must be completed for both spouses.

    Ufile tip

    The software will automatically:

    • calculate the most beneficial split of the eligible pension income
    • generate form T1032, Joint Election to Split Pension Income for both spouses

  • Line 11900 Employment insurance and other benefits (T4E)

    • Canada Emergency Response Benefit (CERB) from Service Canada or any Employment Insurance (EI) benefit payments are taxable.
    • These amounts are shown in box 14 of the T4E slip. Boxes 15, 17, 18, 33, 36 and 37 indicate the type of benefits paid and are included in box 14.
    • If there’s an amount in box 30, the individual repaid either EI benefits, the Canada Emergency Response Benefit (CERB) or a combination of both. If the individual informs you they repaid the CERB, refer to line 23210 Federal COVID-19 benefits repayment. If the individual did not repay the CERB, the repayment amount in box 30 will be claimed on line 23200 in their return.
    • If there is a percentage in box 7 and an amount in box 15 the individual may have to repay an amount.
      • The repayable amount will be reported on line 23500 Social benefits repayment and line 42200 Social benefits repayment of the tax return

    Ufile tip

    The software will automatically calculate how much EI the individual must repay.

  • Line 12100 Interest and other investment income

    • Interest and other investment income are taxable and must be reported on the tax return. They usually are shown on a T5 slip.
    • Individuals may not receive a T5 slip if the investment income is less than $50, but they must still report this income.
    • Individuals also have to report the interest on any tax refund they received during the year as shown on their notice of assessment or reassessment of any tax year.
    • A joint investment income must be reported in the same ratio as the contribution made by each account holder. If only one individual has deposited all the money in a joint account, this individual has to report all investment income from that account.

    Screen by screen instructions: Interest and other investment income

  • Line 12500 Registered disability savings plan (RDSP) income (T4A box 131)

    • An RDSP is taxable and must be reported in the tax return.
    • The taxable amount is found in box 131 of a T4A slip.

  • Line 12700 Taxable capital gains (Sale of principal residence)(T2091)

    • Generally, a volunteer should not prepare returns for individuals who sold their property. CVITP volunteers can prepare returns for individuals who have sold a property only if:
    • the entire property is designated as their principal residence for all years that they owned it
    • the individual never reported any rental income (or loss) generated from the property
    • When an individual sells their principal residence, any capital gain from that sale is not taxable because of the principal residence exemption.
    • The individual must report the sale and designate their property as their principal residence on Schedule 3 and form T2091 – Designation of a property as a principal residence.
    • You need the following information from the individual to report and designate the sale of their principal residence:
      • the date of acquisition
      • the date of disposition
      • the proceeds of disposition
      • the address of the principal residence
      • the percentage of co-ownership if owned jointly with another person (individuals normally co-own 50% with their spouse or common-law partner)
    • The software will automatically report the sale and designation. Due to the exemption, the software will not report any income (capital gains) in the tax return.

    Screen by screen instructions: Taxable capital gains (Sale of principal residence)(T2091)

  • Line 12900 Registered retirement savings plan (RRSP) income (T4RSP)

    • Annuities or money withdrawn from an RRSP are taxable and must be reported on the tax return.
    • The amounts are shown in boxes 16 and 22 on a T4RSP slip.
    • The amount in box 16 may qualify for the Elected split-pension on lines 11600 and 21000. The amount will qualify and the software will automatically claim up to $2,000 for the Pension income amount on line 31400 if one of the following applies:
      • the individual is 65 years of age or older on December 31
      • regardless of their age, received the amount upon the death of their spouse or common-law partner (the individual must provide the volunteer this information)

    Complex situation

    • If there are amounts in boxes other than 16, 22 or 30, it is too complex for the CVITP
    • If box 24 is ticked or there is a SIN in box 36, it is too complex for the CVITP
    • If the individual informs the volunteer that the amount in box 22 was transferred to another RRSP (or another registered plan) or was withdrawn because they had too much money in their RRSP, it is too complex for the CVITP

  • Line 13010 Scholarships, fellowships, bursaries and study grants (T4A box 105)

    • Students who receive scholarships and bursaries (as well as fellowships, study grants and artists’ project grants and awards) will have these amounts reported on a T4A slip. Generally, the income they received will be reported in box 105.
    • For students with full-time enrolment in 2020, 2021, or 2022, this amount is fully exempt
    • For students with part-time enrolment in 2020, 2021, or 2022, the scholarship exemption is equal to the tuition paid, plus the costs of program-related materials, plus the $500 basic scholarship exemption
    • If the student is not a qualifying student and has received an award that is not an artists’ project grant, the exemption is limited to the lesser of $500 or the amount they actually received
    • The software will automatically apply the scholarship exemption, and report the difference on line 13010.

    Screen by screen instructions: Scholarships, fellowships, bursaries and study grants (T4A box 105)

  • Line 13500 Self-employment income (T4A box 048)

    • Generally, a volunteer does not prepare returns for individuals who have self-employment or business income, unless they meet the CVITP eligibility criteria. Refer to About free tax clinics clinics on Canada.ca for more information about tax clinic eligibility criteria.
    • If an individual has a T4A information slip that has Fees for services in box 048, this amount is reported as self-employment or business income and is taxable. When reported, it is mandatory to include form T2125, Statement of Business or Professional Activities with the return.
    • The North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) code is mandatory and must be entered in form T2125. The individual must give you sufficient information so you can find the correct six digit NAICS code.
    • In order for the software to report the income correctly and to generate form T2125, you must enter the income in two different places. First, as business income, then as income from a T4A slip. This does not duplicate the income in the tax return.

    Screen by screen instructions: Self-employment income (T4A box 048)

  • Line 20800 RRSP deduction

    • Individuals who contribute to a registered retirement savings plan (RRSP) may claim a deduction on line 20800.
    • Individuals must confirm their RRSP deduction limit. This amount can be found in their latest notice of assessment under RRSP deduction limit statement, next to the line: Available contribution room for 2022. This amount is also available through AutoFill My Return.
    • Individuals may have two receipts for the same tax year:
      • for contributions made in the last 10 months of the year (March to December)
      • for contributions made in the first 60 days of the following year
    • December 31 of the year the individual turns 71 years of age is the last day that an individual can contribute to their RRSP. Eligible RRSP contributions that have not been deducted, may be carried forward and deducted by the individual in future years, up to the amount of their RRSP deduction limit.
    • The individual must confirm the amount of their contributions, if any, they wish to deduct on their return. Any leftover amount is carried forward. RRSP contributions must be reported even if the individual decides not to claim the deduction this year.
    • The individual might have amounts they have carried forward from previous years. This amount must be entered in the software. This amount can be found in their latest notice of assessment under RRSP deduction limit statement, next to the line: Minus: Unused RRSP contributions previously reported and available to deduct for 2022. This amount is also available through AutoFill My Return
    • The software will generate Schedule 7.

    Screen by screen instructions: RRSP deduction

  • Line 21400 Child care expenses

    • This deduction is an amount an individual paid to have someone look after an eligible child so that they, and their spouse (if applicable), could:
      • earn income from employment
      • carry on a business either alone or as an active partner
      • attend school under the conditions of an educational program
      • carry on research or similar work for which a grant was received
    • Generally, the lower net income earner claims the deduction in their tax return unless a special case exists.
    • Child care – special cases include:
      • attended school and was enrolled in a part-time or full-time educational program
      • was mentally or physically infirm
      • was incarcerated
    • Child care – special cases, is claimed by the higher net income individual or in part by both the higher net income and lower net income individual. In this situation, the person with the higher net income must calculate their claim first because the lower net income individual’s amount will be reduced, entirely or in part, by the amount being claimed by the higher net income individual.

    The software will automatically calculate the amounts based on the number of weeks entered under the higher net income individual’s profile, under Child care – special cases.

    Screen by screen instructions: Child care expenses

  • Line 21500 Disability supports deduction

    • If the individual has an impairment in physical or mental functions, the individual may be able to deduct the eligible expenses that they paid in the year so that they could:
      • work
      • go to school
      • do research for which they received a grant
    • Only the person with the disability can claim expenses for this deduction. They cannot claim:
      • amounts that they, or someone else, claimed as medical expenses (lines 33099 or 33199)
      • amounts for which anyone was reimbursed, or entitled to be reimbursed, by a non-taxable payment, such as insurance for example
    • The individual does not need to have an approved form T2201, Disability Tax Credit Certificate on file with the CRA to be eligible but some specific expenses are eligible only for persons for whom form T2201 has been approved.

  • Line 22900 Other employment expenses (Work from home)

    • Generally, a volunteer should not prepare a return for an individual with employment expenses. However, due to COVID-19, a deduction can be claimed using the Temporary flat rate method if they meet all of the following conditions:
      • they worked more than 50% of the time from home for a period of at least four consecutive weeks in the year due to the COVID-19 pandemic
      • they are only claiming home office expenses and are not claiming any other employment expenses
      • they were not reimbursed by their employer for home office expenses
    • Individuals who are allowed to claim this deduction can claim a rate of $2 for each day worked from home up to a maximum of $500, to cover all home office expenses
    • Under this temporary flat rate method, individuals are not required to keep or provide supporting documents.
    • This temporary flat rate method is available for the years 2020, 2021 and 2022.

  • Line 23200 Provincial or territorial COVID-19 benefits repayment (T4A box 201)

  • Line 23210 Federal COVID-19 benefits repayment (T4A box 201) (T4E box 30)

    • If an individual repaid federal COVID-19 benefits (CERB, CESB, CRB, CRCB, or CRSB) in 2021 or 2022 that they received in a previous year, they can claim a deduction for the repayment.
    • In the T4A slip, the repayment will show in box 201 and the Payer’s name box must read Canada Revenue Agency.
    • In the T4E slip, the repayment will show in box 30, along with other EI amounts repaid. Service Canada will send the individual a letter showing the exact portion of the amount in box 30 that is CERB repayment.
    • Individuals may claim the deduction for the federal COVID-19 benefits repayment in any of the following ways:
      • on line 23210 of their 2022 return
      • on line 23210 of their 2021 return
      • on both their 2021 and 2022 returns as long as the total amount claimed is not more than the combined amounts reported in box 201 of their T4A slip and the letter from Service Canada
    • The individual needs to confirm how they wish to claim the deduction for the repayment. If the individual wants to claim a deduction on their 2021 return but it has already been assessed, they will have to request an adjustment.

    It is not up to the volunteer to determine in which year the deduction will be more beneficial.

  • Line 25500 Northern residents deductions

    • The northern residents is calculated on form T2222 and claimed on line 25500.
    • To claim northern residents deductions, an individual must have lived permanently, in a prescribed zone (Zone A or Zone B), for at least six consecutive months, beginning or ending in the tax year for which you are preparing the return.
    • If the individual has not lived in a prescribed zone for at least six consecutive months at the time of completing their return, they do not yet qualify. You can complete the individual's return without making the claim and, when they qualify, they can ask the CRA to adjust their return.
    • To determine whether the individual’s residence is in Zone A or Zone B, go to Line 25500 - Northern residents deductions and click Places located in prescribed zones.
    • There are two northern residents deductions:
      • the residency deduction
      • the travel deduction
    • The residency deduction is for having lived in a prescribed zone and is made up of two parts:
      • the basic residency amount can be claimed for the number of days in the year that the individual lived in a prescribed zone
      • the additional residency amount can be claimed for the same number of days that were used to calculate the individual’s basic residency amount if they are the only person claiming the basic residency amount for the dwelling that they lived in during that period.
    • The travel deduction is for costs related to travel that started from a prescribed zone and is made up of two parts:
      • medical travel (unlimited trips in the year)
      • other travel (limited to two trips per year, per person)

    Under proposed changes, the taxpayer or an eligible family member must have taken the trip, even if neither received a taxable travel benefit for that trip.

    Individuals can claim a deduction for travel benefits even if they are not claiming a residency deduction. For example, if an individual’s spouse or common-law partner claims both the basic and the additional residency amounts, the individual can still claim a travel deduction.

    • To calculate the travel deduction for each trip, the individual can use one of the following:
      • the value of the taxable travel benefit received from employment (if any)
      • under proposed changes, a portion of the $1,200 standard amount (this is an amount per person, not per trip)
    • For each trip, the software will automatically claim the lowest of the following three amounts:
      • the value of the taxable travel benefit received from employment (if any) or, under proposed changes, the portion of the individual's or their eligible family member's $1,200 standard amount that is allocated to the trip
      • the total travel expenses paid for the trip using either the detailed or simplified calculation methods
      • the lowest cost quoted for a flight from the airport closest to the individual’s residence to the nearest designated city (even if they did not actually travel by air or to that city). It is the individual's responsibility to get the quote for the lowest return air fare.
    • An individual cannot claim a travel deduction if one of the following applies:
      • they or their eligible family member received or was entitled to receive non-taxable amounts as travel assistance, travel allowance, or reimbursement for travel expenses
      • someone else has already claimed the deduction for travel benefits for this trip on their return
    • If the individual is claiming a deduction for medical travel on form T2222, no one can claim it as a medical expense on their return.
    • For detailed information, including meal and vehicle flat rates, as well as the list of nearest designated cities for the lowest airfare, refer to form T2222, Northern residents deductions.

    Screen by screen instructions: Northern residents deductions

    • This is a non-refundable tax credit for individuals if, at any time in the year, the individual supported their spouse or common-law partner who has a net income from line 23600 of their return (or the amount that it would be if they filed a return) that is less than their basic personal amount.

    • This is a non-refundable tax credit for individuals if, at any time in the year, the individual was single, divorced, separated or widowed and supported an eligible dependant who lived with them, was a resident of Canada, and whose net income from line 23600 of their return (or the amount that it would be if they filed a return) was less than their basic personal amount
    • The software will automatically claim this amount.

    The software will claim this amount automatically.

  • Lines 30425, 30450 and 30500 Canada caregiver amount

    • The Canada caregiver amount is a non-refundable tax credit that can be claimed by individuals who provide support to a/several dependant(s) who depend on them for support because of physical or mental impairment.
    • An eligible dependant is considered to depend on an individual for support if they relied on the individual to regularly and consistently provide some or all of the basic necessities of life, such as food, shelter, and clothing. The dependants for which it can be claimed are:
      • their spouse or common-law partner
      • their or their spouse's or common-law partner's child or grandchild
      • their or their spouse's or common-law partner's parent, grandparent, brother, sister, uncle, aunt, niece, or nephew (if resident in Canada at any time in the year)
    • If eligible the Canada caregiver amount may be claimed on:
    • Line 30300 – Spouse or common-law partner amount
      • it will be included as a supplement to the amount already being claimed
    • Line 30400 – Amount for an eligible dependant
      • it will be included as a supplement to the amount already being claimed
    • Line 30425 – Canada caregiver amount for spouse or common-law partner, or eligible dependant age 18 or older
      • an amount will be claimed only if the dependant can be claimed on either line 30300 or 30400
    • Line 30450 – Canada caregiver amount for other infirm dependants age 18 or older

      • there is no requirement that the dependant needs to live with the individual but the dependant’s net income must not exceed $24,604.
      • this amount can be split between individuals but the total amount claimed by both individuals cannot exceed the maximum amount of the credit
    • Line 30500 Canada caregiver amount for infirm children under 18 years of age
      • Refer to Line 30500 – Canada caregiver amount for infirm children under 18 years of age on Canada.ca for more information on claiming the credit for children who do not live with both parents throughout the year, who are in shared custody, or for whom support payments are paid
    • The Canada Revenue Agency may ask for a signed statement from a medical practitioner showing when the impairment began and what its duration is expected to be. The individual does not need a signed statement from a medical practitioner if the CRA already has an approved Form T2201, Disability Tax Credit Certificate, for a specified period.

  • Lines 31600 and 31800 Disability tax credit (T2201)

    • The disability tax credit (DTC) is a non-refundable tax credit that helps persons with disabilities or their supporting family members reduce the amount of income tax they may have to pay.
    • The DTC includes a supplement for persons who are under 18 years of age at the end of the year.
    • To claim the disability amount, the individual must have an approved form T2201 Disability Tax Credit Certificate on file with the CRA.
    • Eligibility criteria to claim the DTC from a dependant on line 31800 require that the dependant:
      • was resident in Canada at some point in the tax year
      • depended on the individual for some or all of the basic necessities of life (food, shelter, or clothing)
    • In addition, one of the following conditions must be met:
      • the individual claimed or could have claimed an amount on line 30400 for the dependant, if the individual did not have a spouse or common-law partner, and if the dependant did not have any income
      • the individual claimed or could have claimed an amount on line 30450 for the dependant, if the dependant had no income and had been 18 years of age or older in 2021
    • An individual cannot claim the disability amount transferred from a dependant for:
      • a child for whom they had to pay child support
      • a person whose spouse or common-law partner claimed the disability amount transferred from a spouse or any other non-refundable tax credit (other than a credit for medical expenses) for that person with a disability
    • If the individual or anyone else paid for attendant care or for care in a facility, the individual may be able to claim the DTC and the amount paid as medical expenses. Refer to Claiming both attendant care as medical expenses and the disability amount, and the certification needed.
    • The software will automatically claim the maximum amount (including the supplement for persons who are under 18) and claim it on line 31600 – Disability amount for self.
    • If the individual with the disability does not need the full amount of this credit to reduce their tax owing, the software will automatically transfer the remaining amount as follows:
      • on line 31800 – Disability amount transferred from a dependant
      • on line 32600 – Amounts transferred from your spouse or common-law partner

    T2201 Disability Tax Credit Certificate

    • Form T2201, Disability Tax Credit Certificate can be submitted online or by mail and must be completed by a medical practitioner, confirming that the individual had a severe and prolonged impairment in physical or mental function during the taxation year.
    • If an individual is claiming the DTC and they do not already have an approved T2201 on file for the year with CRA, it is in the individual’s best interest to submit the return without claiming the DTC and send the T2201 form separately.
    • The T2201 form has a section allowing the individual to specify whether they would like the CRA to adjust their tax returns to reflect the disability amount for themselves or their dependant under 18 years of age, if eligibility is approved. After the application is approved, CRA will automatically adjust their tax returns for all applicable years. The CRA will include the federal and provincial disability amounts for all years that apply. On the other hand, the individual must send an adjustment request if:
      • they are claiming the disability amount for a dependant 18 or older
      • they are claiming the disability amount for their spouse or common law partner
      • the disability amount needs to be divided between two or more supporting persons
    • As a volunteer, you can explain to individuals that Part A of the form is straightforward to complete. They should have it filled in before meeting with their qualified medical practitioner, while Part B is to be completed by their qualified medical practitioner.

    It is not a volunteer’s responsibility to complete the form T2201. For assistance, individuals can contact the individual income tax enquiries line at 1-800-959-8281.

  • Line 32300 Tuition, education, and textbook amounts (T2202)

    • The tuition tax credit is a non-refundable tax credit that students may claim on their income tax and benefit return to reduce the tax they might have to pay.
    • The federal tuition tax credit is calculated using Schedule 11 on the student’s tax return.
    • Eligible tuition fees
      • Generally, a course qualifies for a tuition tax credit if the course is at the post-secondary level and was taken at a post-secondary educational institution. The educational institution provides the student with an official tax receipt or Form T2202, Tuition and Enrolment Certificate
      • Courses that are not at the post-secondary school level are eligible for the tuition tax credit if individuals are 16 years of age or older before the end of the year and are enrolled in the educational institution to obtain skills for, or improve their skills in, an occupation
      • To qualify, the fees paid to attend each educational institution must be more than $100
    • Individuals cannot claim the tuition amount found on their tax certificate or official tax receipt if any of the following applies to them:
      • the fees were paid or reimbursed by their employer, or an employer of one of their parents, where the amount is not included in their or their parent's income
      • the fees were paid by a federal, provincial, or territorial job training program, where the amount is not included in the individual’s income
      • the fees were paid (or eligible to be paid) under a federal program to help athletes, where the payment or reimbursement has not been included in the individual’s income
    • Current year’s unused tuition fees (transfer or carry forward amount)
      • If the current year’s tuition fees are not necessary to reduce the student’s taxes owing, they may choose to transfer or carry forward their unused tuition fees.
      • To designate the transfer, students must complete the transfer section of the T2202.

        Note

        Amounts carried forward cannot be transferred to anyone in the future

      • The student must confirm what they wish to do with their current year’s unused tuition fees:
        • transfer up to a maximum of $5,000 to one individual, such as their spouse or common-law partner, their or their spouse’s or common-law partner’s parent or grandparent
        • carry forward the amount
        • a combination of a transfer and carry forward
      • The transferred amount can be claimed:
        • on line 32600 of their spouse’s or common-law partner’s tax return
        • on line 32400 of their parent or grandparent’s tax return
    • Unused federal and provincial tuition and education amounts from the previous year
      • The student’s unused federal and provincial tuition and education amounts can be found on their notice of assessment from the prior year or by accessing their My Account. It can also be found using AutoFill my Return.

    Previous years information from latest to earliest

    • Prior to 2019, Form T2202, Tuition and Enrolment Certificate was titled T2202A.
    • The federal education and textbook amounts were eliminated on January 1, 2017; however, this measure did not eliminate the tuition tax credit. It does not affect the ability to carry forward unused education and textbook credit amounts from years before 2017.

    Ufile tip

    If the student doesn’t confirm the amount they wish to transfer to their parent, grandparent or spouse, the software will automatically calculate and transfer the amount needed.

    The software will automatically claim any unused federal and provincial tuition and education amounts from the previous year.

  • Lines 33099 and 33199 Medical expenses

    • The medical expense tax credit is a non-refundable tax credit that individuals can use to reduce the tax that they paid or may have to pay.
    • Individuals can claim eligible medical expenses paid for in any 12-month period ending in the tax year you’re filing for and not claimed in prior years. For example: January 2022 to December 2022, or March 2021 to February 2022.
    • Conditions to help you determine if the receipt is an eligible medical expense:
      • The date it was paid must fall within the 12-month period provided. When claiming expenses for all family members, it is important to use the same 12‑month period.
      • Only the part of the expense that has not and will not be reimbursed can be claimed. For example, an amount that has been covered by a medical plan cannot be claimed. You may enter the portion not reimbursed in the software.
      • If the individual has any of these Eligible expenses for the Disability Supports Deduction, refer to Line 21500 – Disability supports deduction to see if they are eligible to claim the expense as a deduction instead of a non-refundable tax credit.
      • Verify if the expense is eligible or needs a prescription in the List of common medical expenses.
      • If the receipt shows payment for medical services by medical practitioners, go to the list that identifies the health care professionals recognized by the Canada Revenue Agency as Authorized medical practitioners for the purposes of the medical expenses tax credit.
    • Travel expenses: individuals can claim travel expenses as a medical expense if equivalent medical services were not available near their home. The individual can claim:
      • the cost of the public transportation expenses (for example, taxis, bus, or train) when they need to travel at least 40 kilometres (one way), but less than 80 km, from their home to get medical services
      • the cost of the travel expenses, including vehicle expenses, accommodations, meals, and parking, when they need to travel at least 80 kilometres (one way) from their home to get medical services
      • refer to Meal and vehicle rates used to calculate travel expenses on how to calculate meals and vehicle expenses
    • Attendant care is care given by an attendant who does personal tasks which a person cannot do for themselves. This includes care in certain types of facilities such as a nursing home or retirement homes. For more information and documents needed to support attendant care expenses, refer to Attendant care and care in a facility. Certain requirements must be met when claiming the disability tax credit (DTC) and attendant care as medical expenses. Refer to Form T2201 in the chart Claiming both attendant care as medical expenses and the disability amount, and the certification needed.

      • their or their spouse's or common-law partner's parents, grand-parents, brothers, sisters, aunts, uncles, nieces, or nephews who were residents of Canada at any time in the year

  • Line 45350 Canada training credit (CTC)

    • The Canada training credit (CTC) is a refundable tax credit intended to help Canadians with the cost of training fees.
    • An eligible individual can accumulate $250 each year toward their Canada training credit limit (CTCL), up to a maximum of $5,000 in a lifetime. The CTCL can be found on their notice of assessment from the prior year. It can also be found through AutoFill my Return.
    • An individual can claim the CTC for eligible tuition and other fees paid for courses taken in the year if they meet all of these conditions:

      • the individual's latest notice of assessment or reassessment shows a Canada training credit limit (CTCL)

      • the tuition and fees are eligible for the tuition tax credit
    • The student will need to confirm the amount they wish to claim if they want to claim an amount lower than their CTCL limit.

      • 2019 is the first year individuals began to accumulate the Canada training credit limit (CTCL)
